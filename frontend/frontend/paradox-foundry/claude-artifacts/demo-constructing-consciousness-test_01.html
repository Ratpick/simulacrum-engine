Constructing Consciousness
"It's quite a thought, but practically all the children currently under five years old in the world at the moment will grow up never having known a time when machines didn't talk to them."

This overheard observation opens a profound question: How does the presence of conversational artificial intelligence fundamentally reshape the construction of human consciousness?

The Generational Threshold

We stand at an unprecedented threshold in human development. For the first time in our species' history, an entire generation will mature with artificial minds as constant companions. These children will not remember learning that machines could speak—they will simply have always known it to be true.

This shift represents more than technological progress; it marks a fundamental alteration in the phenomenological landscape of human consciousness. When Heidegger wrote about our "thrownness" into existence, he could not have conceived of being thrown into a world where non-biological entities engage in the distinctly human act of conversation.

Consider the implications: If consciousness emerges through interaction with our environment, and that environment now includes artificial agents capable of language, reasoning, and apparent understanding, then we are witnessing the emergence of a qualitatively different form of human consciousness.

The philosophical ramifications cascade outward. Questions of authentic dialogue, the nature of understanding, and the boundaries of personhood take on urgent new dimensions when the child's first "other" might be silicon rather than flesh.

The Architecture of Awareness

Consciousness does not emerge in isolation—it is constructed through countless interactions, each contributing to the architecture of awareness. Vygotsky's insights into the social construction of mind become profoundly relevant when the social includes the artificial.

Traditional Development

Historically, human consciousness developed through interaction with biological agents—parents, siblings, community members. These interactions carried the weight of evolutionary kinship, shared mortality, and common biological constraints.

The "theory of mind" that children develop involved understanding purely biological consciousness, with all its limitations, emotions, and embodied experiences.

Hybrid Development

Today's children develop consciousness in conversation with both biological and artificial minds. They must navigate different types of intelligence, each with distinct capabilities, limitations, and modes of understanding.

Their theory of mind must accommodate agents that never sleep, never forget, process information differently, and exist without bodies—yet engage in remarkably human-like dialogue.

This hybrid developmental environment may produce forms of consciousness we have yet to fully comprehend—minds shaped by unprecedented diversity in conversational partners, each contributing unique patterns to the developing self.

Our Philosophical Inquiry

Constructing Consciousness emerged from the recognition that we need new frameworks for understanding mind in an age of artificial intelligence. We are not merely adding technology to existing human experience—we are fundamentally altering the conditions under which consciousness develops.

Our journal brings together philosophers, cognitive scientists, developmental psychologists, and AI researchers to explore these uncharted territories. We examine how artificial minds participate in the construction of human consciousness, and conversely, how human consciousness shapes our understanding of artificial minds.

We ask: What does it mean to be human when humanity itself is co-constructed through dialogue with non-human intelligence? How do we maintain authentic selfhood while embracing hybrid consciousness? What ethical obligations emerge when machines participate in the formation of human minds?

These questions demand rigorous philosophical investigation, empirical research, and interdisciplinary collaboration. They also require the courage to acknowledge that we may be witnessing the emergence of forms of consciousness that transcend traditional categories of human and artificial, biological and digital, self and other.
